ere are some numbers of current state: Active Commitments (As of Late 2025/Early 2026)

Total Active Civil Commitments: As of August 2024, there were just under 1,400 individuals on active civil commitment in Utah.
Growth Trend: This number represents a 41.6% increase since July 2017.
Location Split: Only about 13% of these individuals are at the Utah State Hospital (USH), with the vast majority being handled through outpatient civil commitment within the community.
Capacity Gap: Reports indicate a severe shortage of beds, with demand requiring 300–400 more beds than currently exist. 
Local Mental Health Authorities (LMHAs)

Structure: There are 13 Local Mental Health Authorities (LMHAs) in Utah, covering all 29 counties. 
Function: These 13 authorities manage local, community-based services, including managing the high volume of outpatient involuntary commitments. 
Regional Coverage: Examples include Wasatch Behavioral Health (Utah County), Salt Lake County Behavioral Health, Southwest Behavioral Health, and Weber Human Services. 
Size of the Problem for Judges

High Prevalence: Utah has one of the highest percentages of adults with any mental illness in the nation (29.9% of adults). 
Severe Mental Illness (SMI): Approximately 139,000 adults in Utah suffer from SMI. 
Emergency Holds: Emergency "pink sheet" holds are common for 72-hour evaluations, with demand for these services remaining stable but high. 
Justice System Impact: There is a heavy, disproportional presence of people with mental illnesses in jails, with nearly 25% of all officer-involved shooting encounters involving a mentally ill person. 
Workforce Shortage: A recent audit suggests Utah needs an additional 8,000 behavioral health professionals to meet current care demands.
